# 2025 IELTS Writing Task 2 Topic Analysis

Source folder: 2025 January-December Task 2 model essay collections.

## Executive Summary

The 2025 set contains 39 Task 2 questions. The year is broad, but not random. The strongest repeated patterns are:

1. Education is the largest and most reusable cluster.
2. Culture, history, art, architecture, and language appear repeatedly across the year.
3. Environment is often linked to consumption, food, tourism, fashion, and resource movement.
4. Technology is usually tested through human consequences: work skills, relationships, business meetings, food production, and space exploration.
5. Government questions are rarely isolated; they appear as funding, regulation, enforcement, or public spending inside other topics.
6. Society questions often focus on family relationships, values, competition, freedom, gifts, and lifestyle change.
7. Transport appears less often than in early 2026, but when it appears it is tied to resources, business travel, cities, tourism, or road safety.

## Main Trends In 2025

### 1. Education Is Tested Through Concrete Classroom Decisions

Education questions are not only about "education is important". They ask about specific choices:

- maths vs other subjects
- group study vs self-study
- rewarding top students vs improving students
- teachers' moral role vs academic-only role
- business education vs history
- language as a route into culture

Collocation implication: generate education cards around curriculum design, learning methods, assessment, motivation, and student character, not just schools and exams.

### 2. Culture And Heritage Are A Stable High-Frequency Area

Culture appears through history, old buildings, local housing styles, art galleries, public art, and language. This is one of the clearest annual patterns.

Collocation implication: Arts should be expanded beyond "artistic expression" to include:

- heritage preservation
- historical buildings and monuments
- traditional architectural styles
- public access to cultural institutions
- cultural identity and language learning

### 3. Environment Is Often Attached To Consumption And Lifestyle

The environmental questions in 2025 are less about abstract climate change and more about everyday systems:

- fast fashion and overconsumption
- transporting resources
- vegetarian diets
- protecting animals and birds
- scientific vs natural food production
- tourism in untouched natural places

Collocation implication: Environment cards should include consumption, supply chains, food systems, biodiversity, tourism pressure, and resource use.

### 4. Technology Questions Ask "What Does It Do To Human Life?"

Technology appears as a force that changes relationships, work, business travel, food production, and public spending. The test is less about devices and more about consequences.

Collocation implication: Technology cards should be built around impacts:

- weaken or strengthen social relationships
- improve workplace efficiency
- replace or supplement face-to-face interaction
- transform food production
- justify or waste public investment

### 5. Government Language Is A Cross-Topic Tool

Government appears in funding, enforcement, bans, minimum legal age, public spending, environmental protection, and cultural investment.

Collocation implication: create a reusable "policy language" pack that works across topics:

- allocate public funds
- impose or lift restrictions
- enforce existing laws
- regulate harmful content
- subsidise public activities
- balance public interest and individual freedom

### 6. Society Questions Often Use A Values Conflict

Many 2025 society questions are built on value pairs:

- money vs personal gifts
- freedom vs boundaries
- competition vs cooperation
- family closeness vs modern lifestyle
- entertainment freedom vs social harm

Collocation implication: create contrast frames for moral and social essays, such as "place greater emphasis on", "undermine interpersonal bonds", "foster a more cooperative mindset", and "weaken shared social values".

## 2025 To Early 2026 Trend Comparison

The 2026 January-May set has 14 questions. Compared with 2025, the early 2026 pattern looks more concentrated.

| Area | 2025 pattern | Early 2026 pattern | Trend |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Education | Classroom subjects, study methods, rewards, teacher roles | University vs work, adult literacy, parenting education, rural education access | Moves from classroom micro-issues to social mobility and national development |
| Culture/history | Buildings, monuments, art, language, history vs business | Traditional architecture, historical sites, researching the past | Remains highly stable and should be treated as a priority |
| Transport/urban | City population, local resources, business travel, road age | Congestion, public transport, road law violations, air travel, rural-urban migration | Becomes much more policy-heavy and mobility-focused in 2026 |
| Environment | Fashion, food, animals, natural tourism, resources | Air travel, resource use, public transport, emissions | Shifts toward transport-related environmental arguments |
| Technology | Relationships, work skills, space, food production, business meetings | International business technology, consumer information, air-travel innovation | Becomes more embedded in economy and information rather than standalone tech debate |
| Media | Good news, violent media regulation | Advertising influence, news relevance | Moves from content regulation to information trust and practical relevance |
| Society/family | Freedom, gifts, family relationships, competition | Permissive parenting, youth crime | Narrows toward parenting consequences and youth behaviour |
| Government | Funding, enforcement, bans, legal age | Public transport funding, road enforcement, building rules, adult literacy spending | Continues as a recurring argument mechanism |

## What This Suggests For Future Questions

These are not predictions of exact questions. They are reusable pressure points that recur across the dataset.

1. Expect culture/history to keep returning in different forms: old buildings, historical sites, cultural identity, language, museums, art funding.
2. Expect transport and urban policy to stay important after early 2026: congestion, public transport, road safety, rural-urban movement, air travel.
3. Expect education questions to connect more strongly to society and economy: employability, literacy, practical skills, social responsibility, unequal access.
4. Expect environment questions to be paired with another topic rather than appear alone: fashion, tourism, food, aviation, local resources, consumer products.
5. Expect technology to appear as a tradeoff topic: convenience vs relationships, efficiency vs face-to-face contact, innovation vs cost, automation vs human judgement.
6. Expect government questions to ask for balanced policy, not extreme answers: spend selectively, regulate proportionately, enforce effectively, protect freedom where possible.

## Collocation Generation Notes

For each pack, generate three layers:

1. Topic nouns: concrete concepts students can insert into arguments.
2. Argument verbs: what governments, individuals, companies, parents, schools, or media do.
3. Sentence frames: reusable cause, consequence, contrast, concession, and policy-solution structures.

High-value cross-topic verbs:

- preserve, regulate, enforce, subsidise, allocate, restrict, encourage
- undermine, strengthen, foster, reduce, exacerbate, address, promote
- prioritise, balance, justify, impose, facilitate, transform, discourage

High-value cross-topic adjectives:

- sustainable, accessible, excessive, practical, long-term, balanced
- traditional, historical, cultural, harmful, effective, evidence-based
- sedentary, violent, competitive, cooperative, technology-driven, resource-intensive
